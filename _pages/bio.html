My childhood and teenage years were spent in an enclosed campus in southern China where afterwards I attended undergrad physics. I made a drastic turn after many struggles in physics, spending a number of sobering years learning about psychology of human speech perception as a lab assistant in Hong Kong. I later moved to the UK in search of a clearer path for meaningful contributions.

Starting from working with typically listening individuals, I have gradually been trying to learn how speech perception/processing might go wrong or become effortful in clinical individuals. For MSc I learnt from Prof Patricia Cowell about how speech perception and production are affected by hormones. My PhD (with Prof Peter Howell) studied speech listening in noisy environments in people with age-related hearing loss. A grad school cross-disciplinary training fund kindly sponsored by Prof Ilias Tachtsidis enabled me to develop multimodal brain imaging skills to apply on both typical and clinical individuals. I later worked as an NIHR-funded postdoc with Prof Douglas Hartley studying neural synchrony between typically listening or cochlear-implanted children and their mothers ('inter-brain synchrony'), and most recently under Dr Holly Robsonâ€™s MRC Clinician Scientist Fellowship studying neural speech tracking in people who suffer from aphasia after experiencing a stroke. I am still parts of both teams overseeing neuroimaging analyses in aphasic and cochlear-implanted individuals, whilst currently funded by a Wellcome ECA fellowship on audiovisual speech processing in adult cochlear implant users, working with Dr Matt Davis and co-advised by Dr Bob Carlyon and Prof Hartley.

I am a native Cantonese-Mandarin bilingual. I have been a Scottish resident since the pandemic and practice work-life balance by commuting across River Tweed and northeast England.
